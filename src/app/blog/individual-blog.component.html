The Technological Solution for Aging Water Infrastructure

Utilizing the vast amount of data currently being collected, water utilities and wastewater treatment facilities can increase the resiliency of water infrastructure.

by Jeff Pawlak

If you haven’t noticed, times are tough for water facilities these days. Infrastructure is underfunded. Pumps are breaking. Pipes are bursting. The workforce is aging. And that’s just the start of it.

Most people don’t pay attention to it, but water infrastructure is the very foundation of society. If our water facilities don’t function properly, businesses can’t operate. Families don’t have access to clean drinking water. Farmers can’t water their crops. Our ability to move clean water so reliably to so many people is miraculous, but we can’t afford to take it for granted.

We didn’t arrive at the current situation because of the fault of the water engineers. To the contrary, the engineers deserve all the credit in the world. It isn’t easy working with aging facilities, and things would be a lot worse without their talent and experience in operations.

Reality check: water infrastructure is expensive. So expensive, in fact, that it is three times as capital intensive as other utilities. Not to mention that water is undervalued and underpriced. Rate increases are needed to finance large new facilities, but if the political will in a community is opposed to paying more, it puts the future of new facilities in jeopardy.

We’ve seen what the worst case scenario looks like for water utilities when finances go wrong. The situation in Flint, Michigan arose because the city went bankrupt, and didn’t have the money to keep buying clean water from Detroit. So the local officials started pulling water from the polluted Flint River without proper treatment facilities, leading to biological contamination, and, ultimately, lead poisoning in the community.

The American Society of Civil Engineers called for an investment of over a trillion dollars in water infrastructure over the next 25 years to ensure integrity of systems. In all likelihood, this won’t happen. Despite bipartisan support and popular opinion polls broadly supporting major investment in infrastructure, there is no evidence of an improvement in the situation. The Heritage Foundation noted that the current backlog of projects in the Army Corps of Engineers stands at $96 Billion; the recent water infrastructure law passed by Congress and signed into law will only add to the backlog.

There is no solution for comprehensive investment at a local, state, and federal level. But even absent of political leadership, there is no reason to write off our facilities. Because there is still a lot that we can do to improve efficiency and operations. And we can invest in technology that drastically improves the way that we operate water infrastructure facilities in the United States, with better results for less money than ever before.

The technological solutions are largely related to data, software, and artificial intelligence. Water utilities sit on massive datasets - including SCADA systems, GIS, billing, facility energy consumption, break reports, and more. More modern facilities are installing Advanced Metering Infrastructure - sophisticated systems to capture enormous amounts of data on customer demand and usage.

That data is hugely valuable when paired with machine learning (ML) algorithms. ML can predict and prevent facility downtime, improve water quality, save money, defer asset investment, and so much more.

This is important because most water utilities collect a lot of data, and aren’t using it. It is waiting to be processed and analyzed, and when that happens, the positive results could be staggering. Machine learning can improve bottom-line business results by as much as 20%.

We may not have the money to replace our facilities (yet). But in the meantime, there is an unbelievable amount of potential in datasets to increase the resiliency of water infrastructure. It just needs to be paired with the algorithms, and the machine learning will do the rest of the work. More efficient operations, reduced risk, long term business value.
